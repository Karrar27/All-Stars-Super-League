Transfers & Arrivals 26/27 Preview · Pre-Season

The New Face on the Touchline: Ammar Set to Make His All Stars Bow

A familiar name from outside the league's footballing circles is stepping into the dugout — and both Nabil and Karrar know exactly what's coming.

It's not often that a single pre-season announcement sends a ripple through every corner of the league's group chat — but news of Ammar's impending debut in the 26/27 season has done exactly that.

Ammar is no stranger to competitive battles, even if his name hasn't yet appeared on an All Stars Super League scoresheet. His history with both Nabil and Karrar stretches back to ventures well outside football — shared projects, shared rivalries, and by all accounts, more than a few scores still considered unsettled. Those who know him describe a competitor who treats every contest, regardless of the arena, with the same intensity.

On the FPL side, Ammar's reputation precedes him. Sources close to the new arrival describe a "decent" track record — the kind of understated phrase that, in this league, usually means someone has quietly been finishing in the top brackets of their previous leagues for years without making much noise about it.

"He's calm, he's calculated, and he already knows how Nabil thinks and how Karrar plans. That's not a great combination for either of them." — A source close to the new arrival

With the league set to expand for 26/27 (more on that elsewhere in this issue), Ammar's entrance adds another genuine contender to an already crowded field. Karrar, fresh off his own vow to chase silverware next season, and Nabil, still nursing the wounds of a Draft League collapse, may find their first major test of the new campaign arriving from somewhere much closer to home than they expected.

The All Stars Super League newsroom will have a full profile on Ammar — including his squad-building philosophy and opening transfers — once pre-season draft activity begins.

Season Review 25/26 · Final Review

Hameed's Treble Season: Inside a Record-Breaking Campaign

Three titles, a host of shattered records, and a campaign that will be discussed for years — the numbers behind the most dominant individual season the league has seen.

There are good seasons, there are great seasons, and then there is whatever Hameed just produced. By the time the curtain fell on 25/26, the youngest manager in the league had claimed the Draft League title, the H2H Main title, and the Classic title — a clean sweep that the All Stars Super League has never seen from a single manager before.

The headline number tells its own story: Hameed spent 35 gameweeks in 1st position across the Draft League this season — more than triple the previous benchmark, and a tally that effectively turned the title race into a formality for most of the campaign.

Even the league's record book had to be rewritten in places. The honorary Double Gameweek Points-on-the-Week mark of 110 points dwarfs anything seen previously, while a 12-match win streak in the Draft League put real distance between Hameed and the rest of the chasing pack before most rivals had even settled into the season.

What makes the campaign feel especially historic is the context: Hameed remains the youngest entrant in the league's history, and just two seasons removed from his introduction to organised FPL. The trajectory from newcomer to treble-winner, inside such a short window, places this season firmly in "legacy" territory — a campaign that future All Stars seasons will likely be measured against.

With three trophies added to his cabinet in a single year and the league heading into an expanded 26/27 season, the obvious question is whether anyone can find an answer before Hameed's dominance becomes the new normal.

Awards Night 25/26 · Awards Ceremony

"One for the Future" — and a Promise for Right Now: Karrar's Awards Night Vow

The league's founder collected individual hardware for the first time in years — and used the moment to put the rest of the table on notice.

For a man widely regarded as the heart and brain of the All Stars Super League, the trophy cabinet has remained — until now — conspicuously bare. That changed on awards night, when Karrar was named the winner of "One for the Future", the award recognising the manager showing the most promising development and long-term potential.

It's a notable moment for the league's founder. Karrar built the All Stars Super League from a single phone call between two friends into the multi-competition institution it is today, all while continuing to be — by his own admission — one of the league's most tactically literate managers without the silverware to show for it.

"I've helped build the stage for everyone else to win on it. That's been fine — I'm proud of that. But I'm not done chasing my own. Next season, I'm coming for a title. Not 'one for the future' — the real thing." — Karrar, Awards Ceremony Speech, 25/26

The acceptance speech, delivered to a room that has watched Karrar narrowly miss out on major honours season after season, struck a tone that was as much a rallying cry as it was a thank-you. He acknowledged the rivalries that have defined his seasons — long-running battles with Zain, Nabil, Hameed and Akram — and framed the year ahead as the one where those rivalries finally tip in his favour.

It's worth noting that Karrar's 69-point haul — the joint-highest single-gameweek score in the 25/26 Draft League, shared with Hameed — has long stood as proof of his ceiling. The missing piece, by his own admission, has been turning peak weeks into sustained title challenges.

With a new manager arriving in Ammar, an expanded league structure, and Hameed setting the bar at a historic high, Karrar's vow couldn't have come at a more pointed moment. The "One for the Future" award may be the first piece of individual hardware in his cabinet — but after this speech, few in the league will be surprised if it isn't the last.

Season Preview 26/27 · Looking Ahead

A New Era Dawns: All Stars Super League Gears Up for Its Biggest Season Yet

More managers, more competitions, and a Premier League landscape entering a new era of its own — 26/27 is shaping up to be the most significant season in the league's history.

If 25/26 was the season of one manager's historic dominance, early signs suggest 26/27 will be remembered as the season the All Stars Super League itself changed shape.

League organisers are confident that the upcoming season will see record participation numbers, with the confirmed arrival of new manager Ammar already pushing the competitive field to its widest in league history — and further additions reportedly in discussion ahead of the new campaign. More managers means more rivalries, more storylines, and a deeper, more unpredictable table across Draft, H2H Main, Classic and Cup competitions alike.

The timing lines up with what's happening at the very top of the football pyramid. The Premier League itself enters a new era in 26/27, and that shift filters directly down into fantasy football — new dynamics, new breakout players, and shifting tactical trends that every manager in this league will need to adapt to in real time.

"Every time the Premier League changes, FPL changes with it — and this league has always thrived on that chaos. A new era up top means new surprises down here. Nobody's strategy from last season is safe." — League organiser, pre-season briefing

For returning managers, the message is clear: the records, rivalries and reputations built over 24/25 and 25/26 mean little if they can't be defended against a wider, hungrier field. For Hameed, it's a chance to prove last season's treble was the beginning of a dynasty rather than a peak. For Karrar, it's the stage on which he's promised to finally chase a title. For Akram and Nabil, it's an opportunity for redemption after a difficult end to 25/26.

One thing is certain — with a bigger league, a changing Premier League landscape, and several open scores still to settle, 26/27 is shaping up to be the most closely watched season the All Stars Super League has ever run.

Season Review 25/26 · Final Review

The Great Collapse: Akram's Title Defence Falters, Nabil's Draft Dream Unravels

One manager couldn't follow up his maiden title. Another spent 22 gameweeks punching above his weight before the underlying numbers finally caught up. A look back at 25/26's biggest disappointments.

Not every storyline from 25/26 was a celebration. While Hameed swept the board and Karrar earned a place on the podium, two of the league's most established names will look back on the season with frustration — and in one case, genuine heartbreak.

Akram, the reigning Draft League champion from 24/25, came into the season as the man to beat. Instead, his title defence never got going. The same "differential" philosophy that won him his maiden title — bold, against-the-grain selections built on real-world potential — repeatedly backfired, and the season also produced his longest losing streak of 7 matches, a sharp contrast to the 24/25 campaign where he shared the league's best win streak. A manager who once turned bold calls into bragging rights instead found those same calls compounding his problems week after week.

But if Akram's season was a quiet fade, Nabil's was a far louder story altogether. For 22 gameweeks, Nabil sat at or near the very top of the Draft League table — a remarkable feat for a manager making his full-time debut in the competition. On paper, it looked as though the league's newest full-timer was about to claim one of its biggest prizes at the first attempt.

Dig beneath the league position, though, and the shared sentiment among observers — and reportedly among rival managers themselves — was that Nabil was punching well above his weight. By underlying squad quality and total points accumulated, his side's numbers read more like a team destined for 3rd or 4th than one leading the table outright. The consensus was that the position flattered the performance: a manager riding favourable results and matchups to a rank his squad's actual point tallies simply didn't support.

"Twenty-two gameweeks at the top doesn't tell the full story. Everyone could see the underlying numbers — the total points just weren't there for a side meant to be leading. It always felt like a 3rd or 4th place team punching above its weight, and eventually the table caught up with that." — League observer, end-of-season review

And catch up it did. Nabil's season is now home to two unwanted records: the lowest single-gameweek score of the campaign (19 points) and the longest losing streak in H2H Main (10 matches) — both consistent with a squad whose underlying numbers had been overdue a reality check long before gameweek 22. The same hyper-reactive, transfer-heavy approach that had masked the gap between position and points eventually became the undoing, with rebuilds compounding rather than correcting the slide.

By the time the dust settled, Hameed had swept past both of them on the way to a historic treble, leaving Akram to defend a title he never looked like keeping and Nabil to reflect on 22 gameweeks at the top that, by his own squad's numbers, were never quite built to last. For both managers, 26/27 — with its expanded field and new arrivals — represents not just a fresh start, but a chance to answer the only question that matters: was 25/26 a blip, or a pattern?
